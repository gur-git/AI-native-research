# A professor's account of the early steps of the classical research process

## What was observed / claimed

The maintainer relayed notes from a conversation with the pilot team's supervising professor about how the early stages of a research effort are classically conducted and mentored. The professor described his own established mentoring practice and the sequence of steps he expects a new researcher to move through. The notes were recorded by the maintainer from memory, not transcribed; the date above is the recording date, not necessarily the conversation date.

This is the first entry in what is intended to be a recurring stream: occasional conversations with the professor about the proper conduct of research, used as raw material the methodology adapts (not copies) into AI-native form.

## Specific claims

- **Knowledge first.** One of the first steps for a new researcher is to become knowledgeable enough in the field to make insightful observations and hold meaningful conversations. Research moves (observation, conversation, ideation) are gated on this fluency.
- **Explain-back as the progress measure.** The professor's working method for tracking a student's progress: on a weekly basis, the student explains a new concept from the field to him, and he corrects inaccuracies. He reports this as an effective tracking instrument in his own mentoring practice.
- **Then a research proposition, in five parts.** Once knowledgeable enough, the next step is to aim for a research proposition comprising: (1) motivation and background; (2) system model; (3) problem formulation; (4) existing approaches; (5) the final research proposition — what the research will do and how it will contribute.
- **Build an MVP.** He named building a minimal working version of the research artifact early — rather than specifying everything up front — as a step in the sequence. *(The maintainer's own extension, marked as such: this lands harder in the AI regime, since AI collapses build cost — an agile posture fits research better than a waterfall one. That rationale is the maintainer's, not the professor's.)*
- **Discuss the MVP with experts, in parallel.** After the MVP exists, and in parallel with continuing the research (not as a stop-and-wait stage), discuss it with experts in the field and gather their opinions.

## Implications (suggested, not concluded)

- The explain-back practice bears on **A16** (output without reciprocal skill development) and **A13** (the perception gap): it is a classical, field-tested instrument that measures *actual* rather than *felt* understanding, and it directly measures the second output (researcher capacity). It is a concrete candidate answer to open question **Q11** (how to track researcher development). It also converges with a practice the pilot built and exercised (the `quiz` gate — see `../observations/2026-06-10-pilot-workspace-audit.md`); note the convergence is not full independence — this professor supervises that same pilot team, and both sources are recorded via the maintainer.
- The five-part proposition bears on **A4** (selection is the new bottleneck) and **A7** (anchoring): it is a concrete convergence artifact for the survey-then-focus motion, authored by the researcher, that makes the framing explicit before execution. The "existing approaches" part is exactly where **A8** (frontier hallucination) demands source-walking discipline (G10).
- The MVP step bears on **A3** (massive parallelism is cheap) and **A1**: the classical argument for waterfall planning — building is expensive, so plan exhaustively — weakens when AI collapses build cost for the recombination parts. The caveat is **A9**: a cheap MVP still carries an unchanged verification bill.
- The expert-discussion step bears on **A6** (self-validation is structural) and **A8**: in an AI-saturated workflow, an expert human who has not seen the AI session is among the few genuinely independent critics, and is strongest exactly at the frontier where AI is weakest. It also bears on **Q7** (what selective retention looks like for a researcher without a team — the mentor channel is part of the answer).
- These *might* support guidelines along the lines of: an explain-back gate on knowledge-building; a researcher-authored five-part proposition as the focus artifact; an MVP-first execution posture; an early, parallel expert-review loop.

## Notes / caveats

- Single source, relayed secondhand from memory, no transcript, conversation date unrecorded — hence `weak`, even though the claims about his own mentoring practice are likely reliable as descriptions of that practice. n=1 professor in one field culture (EE / signal processing); the five-part proposition structure in particular may be field-specific, and other fields may converge on different artifacts.
- The professor was describing classical (pre-AI) research practice. The methodology's interest is in adapting the underlying functions (knowledge gating, framing artifacts, early contact with reality, independent expert critique) to the AI-native regime, not in transplanting the practices verbatim.
