# Step 0 — Landscape of public positions on research-with-AI

> **About this document.** This is a working artifact from the first step of a literature survey on AI-native research methodology, not a foundation claim and not a finished literature review. The survey's deeper stages (per-territory literature pass, primary reading, integration into the foundation) come later. Step 0 is a shallow public-positions scan whose job is to orient the inquiry — to answer "is the field converged, contested, or scattered?" — before deeper reading begins. URLs were verified at time of subagent fetch but have not been re-verified for the foundation; nothing in this document is citation-grade until it is. A second round on 2026-05-17 broadened coverage to include practicing scientists, heavy AI users, and AI-side actors missed in round 1, integrated each vector's distinctness into its explanation, and added a players appendix. A same-day follow-on pass extended Karpathy's coverage (the LLM OS framework, the vibe-coding origin and walkback, context engineering, Eureka Labs, the Sequoia AI Ascent 2026 piece) and added **Anthropic's published Claude usage guides** as a distinct methodology corpus alongside Anthropic's higher-level positioning. A final small addition prepended an **At a glance** preamble (below) that summarizes the five visions and the bet each makes, so a new reader can orient before reading the detailed treatment.

## At a glance — five visions, five bets

Each approach in the field of AI-for-research makes an implicit wager — the assumption that, if wrong, sinks the approach. The five visions canvassed in this document, with the distinctive move of each and the bet each makes:

1. **Agentic delegation.** *Unique:* AI executes the full research cycle (literature, hypothesis, experiment, paper); the human only enters at problem selection and final evaluation; automated verification (benchmarks, replication, outcome-grading) replaces human inspection. *Bet:* Today's reliability ceilings are temporary, and researcher skill atrophy doesn't matter because the researcher won't need to execute research anyway.

2. **Augmentation with architectural prescription.** *Unique:* Methodology lives in the tool's design — autonomy sliders, source-grounding, embedded expert review, citation transparency — not in the practitioner's discretion. *Bet:* A well-designed tool encodes methodology by structure; the architecture is the right unit of methodology. Get the loop right, and individual skill matters less.

3. **Craft / skill preservation.** *Unique:* The researcher is the constant; AI is one of many instruments; what the researcher *becomes* through repeated AI use is the methodological object. Practice is the unit of methodology. *Bet:* Deliberate practice can outpace the structural pull toward outsourcing — skill formation can be made conscious enough to resist the gradient — and craft transmits across practitioners.

4. **Governance / disclosure / cautionary.** *Unique:* Methodology as rule rather than practice — prohibitions, disclosures, infrastructure prerequisites. In the architectural sub-position (LeCun, Apple): current AI is on the wrong substrate to do science on at all. *Bet:* The right level to manage AI in research is the system (editorial policy, regulation, infrastructure), not the practitioner. Get the rules right and individual practice will sort itself out.

5. **Silence.** *Unique:* Absence by actors with the natural authority to opine. The silence itself shapes practice by leaving it unguided. *Bet:* Varies by silent actor. On the AI side (xAI, Mistral, Cohere): methodology isn't our problem — we build infrastructure, someone else figures out usage. On the metascience side (COS / Nosek): existing integrity frameworks (transparency, preregistration, replication) will adapt to AI without specific guidance, or it is still too early to commit.

**Where this inquiry sits** *(for transparency about the position the rest of the document is written from):* The repo whose survey produced this document proposes a sixth position. *Unique:* Researcher growth is treated as the **positive** design criterion AI use is evaluated against, rather than as a warning to manage. Methodology is constructed (foundation → evidence → guidelines), with every guideline required to articulate the skill it develops, preserves, or risks substituting. No other player inside Vector 3 (craft) has built it as a systematic artifact of this kind. *Bet:* The gap between Karpathy's negative formulation ("you can't outsource understanding") and a positive one ("AI use should grow the researcher") is load-bearing. Methodology can be grown bottom-up from evidence rather than asserted in essays or warnings. The researcher's developing competence is a legitimate first-class output of research practice, alongside the deliverable itself.

The detail behind each vision — what research looks like in practice, the players, the evidence, what each vector does not address — is in §3 below.

## 1. Purpose

To map, at the shallowest useful depth, the public positions of major actors in the AI-for-research space on the question: **how should research be done with AI?**

Three reasons the inquiry needed this:

1. To know whether the niche the repo occupies (positive methodology, individual-researcher scale, evidence-grown, foundation→guidelines structured, empower-not-replace as a design criterion) is already occupied by a major player. If it is, the repo is duplicate work and should pivot.
2. To know which adjacent positions are loud enough to need engagement (adoption, refinement, or contestation) when the foundation is revised.
3. To know what vocabulary the field has already settled on so the repo does not unwittingly reinvent terms.

## 2. Method

Two rounds of parallel subagent canvassing.

**Round 1 (2026-05-16).** Three subagents covered 20 entities across three clusters: frontier labs and autonomous-research systems; augmentation tools and individual voices; institutions, metascience, critics, and venues.

**Round 2 (2026-05-17).** Three subagents added 27 entities across three clusters: (1) Alex Wissner-Gross / PSI in depth (per maintainer request), plus AI-side actors missed in round 1 (Yann LeCun / AMI Labs, xAI, Microsoft AI / Suleyman, Apple ML Research, Mistral, Cohere); (2) practicing scientists with public AI-methodology positions (Terence Tao, Andrew Ng, Jeremy Howard, Eric Topol, George Church, Sébastien Bubeck, Patrick Collison, Lior Pachter, John Carmack); (3) heavy AI users and research tools missed in round 1 (Matt Welsh, Sebastian Raschka, Cassie Kozyrkov, Cal Newport, Hadley Wickham, Patrick Mineault; Consensus, Scite, SciSpace, Causaly, Connected Papers, Litmaps, Iris.ai, Humata, Genei).

For each entity: canonical positioning URL verified by fetch, one-line summary of stated position on how research should be done with AI, classification (augmentation / replacement / cautionary / silence / other), human-role-and-skill-formation note, distinctiveness note, confidence rating. "No explicit methodology position found" was treated as a valid finding rather than a failure.

**Total canvassed: 47 entities.** Limits in §5. Full player list with one-line descriptions and homepages in §7.

## 3. The five vectors found

### Vector 1 — Agentic delegation / "AI does the research"

**One-line.** AI takes over execution of the research cycle (literature, hypothesis, experiment, paper); the human retains problem selection, evaluation, and ethical decisions.

**What separates this vector from the others.** The unit of work is the **project**, not the task — and consequently human oversight is not assumed to scale with AI output. Where the augmentation camp (Vector 2) keeps the human in the loop at every step, delegation accepts that human inspection cannot scale to the volume AI produces and relies on automated verification (benchmarks, outcome-grading, replication) as the trust layer. Where the craft camp (Vector 3) treats AI use as a practice the researcher should develop, delegation treats it as a service to be consumed. The rhetorical surface of this vector is almost always augmentation language ("AI Scientists pair with humans," "tireless companions," "AI as collaborator"); the architecture and the long-term trajectory are not. Round 2 surfaced the sharpest manifestation of this in Wissner-Gross/PSI and Suleyman/Microsoft AI, both of whom hold tactical augmentation and strategic replacement positions simultaneously.

**What research looks like in practice.** A human submits a research area or question. An autonomous agent (or multi-agent system) executes the full pipeline — literature search, hypothesis generation, experimental design, code, sometimes wet-lab execution paired with automation, paper drafting. The human reviews at end of cycle, redirects, or evaluates. Productivity claims are stated in cycle-time compression (FutureHouse: "one day of our AI Scientist accomplishes what human scientists accomplish in 6 months"; Sakana AI Scientist v2 published in Nature, March 2026, after running the full pipeline). Methodology emphasis is on automated verification because human inspection cannot scale to the volume of output.

**Tools associated with this vector.** Sakana AI Scientist (v1, v2), FutureHouse's Robin / Phoenix / Finch / Crow / Owl agents, OpenAI Deep Research, Google DeepMind's AI co-scientist, Anthropic's internal Automated Weak-to-Strong Researcher framework, PSI's Get Physics Done (GPD) — "the first open-source agentic AI physicist," SciSpace (the most automation-maximalist tool in the survey).

**Players and evidence.**

- **OpenAI** — Deep Research framed as doing work "for you independently." Sam Altman: "intern-level research assistant by September 2026," "legitimate AI researcher by 2028." Sources: [introducing-deep-research](https://openai.com/index/introducing-deep-research/), [Altman / 2028 researcher](https://techcrunch.com/2025/10/28/sam-altman-says-openai-will-have-a-legitimate-ai-researcher-by-2028/).
- **Sakana AI** — AI Scientist v2 published in Nature, March 2026. Fully autonomous architecture; human involvement is initial direction + ethical oversight. Source: [ai-scientist-nature](https://sakana.ai/ai-scientist-nature/).
- **FutureHouse** — "Pairs AI Scientists with exceptional human scientists to do science more efficiently than before." Robin (announced 2024) is the end-to-end demonstration. Sources: [futurehouse.org/about](https://www.futurehouse.org/about), [Robin announcement](https://www.futurehouse.org/research-announcements/demonstrating-end-to-end-scientific-discovery-with-robin-a-multi-agent-system).
- **Anthropic — internal** — "Automated Weak-to-Strong Researcher" (alignment team, 2026): automate outcome-gradable problems; free humans for vaguer / riskier ones. Candidly names that "supervising Claude requires the very coding skills that may atrophy from AI overuse." Source: [alignment.anthropic.com — automated-w2s-researcher](https://alignment.anthropic.com/2026/automated-w2s-researcher/).
- **Alex Wissner-Gross / Physical Superintelligence (PSI) / Get Physics Done (GPD)** — Wissner-Gross is a physicist and entrepreneur who co-founded PSI, a vertically integrated company building agentic AI for physics research. PSI's open-source release, GPD, is described as "the first open-source agentic AI physicist." His position is distinctive within the delegation vector for holding two registers simultaneously. At the **tool level**, GPD treats AI agents as "graduate students, not autopilots," with explicit checkpoints, supervised mode, and a stated philosophy favoring "scientific rigor and critical thinking over agreeability" — the most craft-preserving agentic-system design in the survey. At the **macro level**, his *Solve Everything* thesis frames physics as being "steamrolled by well-targeted generalist AIs"; PSI's strategic agenda is "vertical integration" of the full research pipeline into a single compute stack ("the Industrial Intelligence Stack"). The concrete milestones reinforce the macro frame: LBNL's "flawless" replication of a 2023 condensed-matter paper end-to-end (already in [reading-list.md](../reading-list.md)) and the resolution of Erdős conjecture #1196 / the ESS-conjecture before any human did. Sources: [PSI Substack — Physical Superintelligence](https://theinnermostloop.substack.com/p/physical-superintelligence); [Welcome to May 3, 2026](https://theinnermostloop.substack.com/p/welcome-to-may-3-2026); [Solve Everything](https://theinnermostloop.substack.com/p/solve-everything).
- **Microsoft AI / Mustafa Suleyman** — clearest example of the same tactical/strategic tension at the corporate level. Frames AI as "human-centered," "in service of people"; the "Humanist Superintelligence" concept is built on calibrated, contextualized augmentation. Simultaneously predicts AI will reach human-level performance on "most, if not all, professional tasks" within 12-18 months. Both positions held in the same public discourse window. Sources: [Cloud Wars — Humanist Superintelligence](https://cloudwars.com/ai/mustafa-suleyman-outlines-microsofts-vision-for-human-centered-superintelligence/); [Fortune — 18-month automation prediction (Feb 2026)](https://fortune.com/2026/02/13/when-will-ai-kill-white-collar-office-jobs-18-months-microsoft-mustafa-suleyman/).
- **SciSpace** — pitches an "AI Super Agent" that orchestrates the full research workflow with the explicit claim "save 90% of your research time" — the clearest automation-maximalist tool in the survey, with no articulated position on the researcher's role beyond efficiency. Source: [scispace.com](https://scispace.com/).
- **Matt Welsh** — included here with a caveat. His "The End of Programming" (CACM, January 2023) sits adjacent to this vector but argues a distinct variant: that the programmer role itself *transforms* into AI teacher / specification writer / output reviewer rather than being augmented. Where Wissner-Gross and Suleyman accept (or schedule) human replacement, Welsh maps a new human role on the other side of the transition. Source: [cacm — The End of Programming](https://cacm.acm.org/opinion/the-end-of-programming/).

**What this vector does not address.** Whether researcher skill should develop as a goal in itself. The Anthropic W2S candor (skill atrophy named, not resolved) and Wissner-Gross's tactical/strategic split are the closest the vector comes to engaging the question.

### Vector 2 — Augmentation with architectural prescription

**One-line.** AI is positioned as a collaborator, but with explicit architectural rules about the human's place in the loop (verification, supervision, source-grounding, autonomy sliders, embedded expert review).

**What separates this vector from the others.** The workflow is designed around **the human staying in the loop at every step** — not just at the start and end. Source-grounding, citation transparency, autonomy sliders, embedded expert review are architectural choices that prefer friction over scale. What separates this vector from craft (Vector 3) is that the prescription lives in the **tool's design**, not in the researcher's practice; the researcher is expected to use the tool well by following its built-in workflow, rather than by developing skill independent of any tool. Where governance (Vector 4) addresses what AI must not do, this vector engages methodology but locates the answer in the tool architecture.

**What research looks like in practice.** The researcher works in a tool environment with explicit human-AI loop boundaries. AI generates; the human verifies. Each AI action is auditable — citation transparency, reasoning logs visible to the user, outputs grounded only in the source corpus the user provided. Workflow design is a methodological decision: define success criteria up front, build in checkpoints, prefer tools that expose AI reasoning over those that do not, accept friction in exchange for verifiability.

**Tools and methodologies.** Elicit (literature workflow with supervision interfaces and reasoning logs); NotebookLM (source-grounded — AI cannot answer from outside the user's notebook); Anthropic's Claude in agent / projects modes with explicit checkpoint behavior; Karpathy's described workflow ("autonomy sliders," human-as-verifier); Scite.ai (citation context as the unit of research trust); Causaly (embedded human expert review as a product feature); Connected Papers and Litmaps (architecturally anti-hallucination — no LLM synthesis at all in Connected Papers, LLM outputs validated against literature database in Litmaps).

**Players and evidence.**

- **Elicit** (formerly Ought) — "Supervise the process, not the output." Co-founder Jungwon Byun: "people are increasingly going to be put in the position of having to evaluate and be really explicit and mindful of what success looks like before then using AI tools to manifest that." Source: [latent.space — Supervise the Process of AI Research](https://www.latent.space/p/elicit).
- **Andrej Karpathy** — the most operationally articulate single voice in Vector 2, with a body of work that traces a consistent trajectory across paradigm shifts. The lineage runs from "Software 2.0" (Medium, Nov 2017 — neural networks as a new programming paradigm; the programmer's role splits between data curation and infrastructure) to "Software 3.0" (YC AI Startup School keynote, June 2025 — "the hottest new programming language is English") to "agentic engineering" (Sequoia AI Ascent 2026 — orchestrating fallible agents as a professional discipline). Specific contributions:
  - **The "LLM OS" framework** (X, Sept 2023): LLM as kernel process, context window as RAM, retrieval as file system, agents as long-running apps. The conceptual precursor to most subsequent agentic-pattern literature.
  - **"Vibe coding"** (X, Feb 2025): originally a deliberately casual mode for throwaway prototyping ("fully gives in to the vibes, embrace exponentials, and forget that the code even exists"); rapidly appropriated for production use, then clarified in his Feb 2026 retrospective as "a shower of thoughts throwaway tweet" not intended as a professional prescription. The cleanest illustration in the survey of a methodology term being misread as it crossed audiences.
  - **"Context engineering" over "prompt engineering"** (X, 2025): names the developer's primary craft as "the delicate art and science of filling the context window with just the right information for the next step." Reinforces an empower frame — the developer must become expert in model-state management.
  - **Eureka Labs** (launched July 2024): his AI-native education startup. Among Vector 2 voices, the most explicit institutional embodiment of empower-not-replace — human expert teachers design materials, AI delivers them at scale and in all languages, the teacher's epistemic authority is non-negotiable.
  - **Sequoia AI Ascent 2026** (April 2026): the most precise formulation in the entire survey of the empower-not-replace boundary — **"You can outsource your thinking, but you can't outsource your understanding."** Names the irreducible developer skills explicitly: system design judgment, spec and plan creation, code review and aesthetic judgment, understanding of fundamental concepts (tensor storage, memory efficiency), security threat assessment. The "verifiability thesis" (LLMs automate what can be verified faster than what can merely be specified) is also distinctive.

  Karpathy sits in Vector 2 because his most public framings are architectural (Software 2.0/3.0, autonomy sliders, LLM OS), but his agentic-engineering work crosses substantially into Vector 3 — the irreducible-skills emphasis is a craft argument. Among AI-lab voices, his recent work is the closest cousin to the repo's "empower, not replace" framing; the gap is that he frames it negatively (what you must not outsource) where the repo frames it positively (what AI use should grow).

  Sources: [Software 2.0 (Medium, 2017)](https://karpathy.medium.com/software-2-0-a64152b37c35); [Software Is Changing (Again) transcript (YC, 2025)](https://singjupost.com/andrej-karpathy-software-is-changing-again/); [LLM OS thread (X, Sept 2023)](https://x.com/karpathy/status/1707437820045062561); [vibe coding origin (X, Feb 2025)](https://x.com/karpathy/status/1886192184808149383); [context engineering endorsement (X, 2025)](https://x.com/karpathy/status/1937902205765607626); [Eureka Labs / TechCrunch coverage (July 2024)](https://techcrunch.com/2024/07/16/after-tesla-and-openai-andrej-karpathys-startup-aims-to-apply-ai-assistants-to-education/); [Sequoia AI Ascent 2026 — agentic engineering](https://karpathy.bearblog.dev/sequoia-ascent-2026/).
- **NotebookLM (Google)** — tagline "AI Research Tool and Thinking Partner." Source-grounding is the core architectural commitment: AI stays within the user-provided corpus. No deeper methodology manifesto. Source: [notebooklm.google](https://notebooklm.google/).
- **Google DeepMind** — Hassabis frames AI as "tool that could help the best scientists in the world make discoveries," lists the human's role as: select problems, interpret what AI does, decide what to deploy. Source: [IAS talk, May 2025](https://blog.biocomm.ai/2025/06/05/sir-demis-hassabis-on-the-future-of-knowledge-institute-for-advanced-study/); co-scientist paper: [arXiv 2502.18864](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.18864).
- **Anthropic — public** — "How scientists are using Claude" (already in [reading-list.md](../reading-list.md)) and the AI for Science Program: [anthropic.com/news/ai-for-science-program](https://www.anthropic.com/news/ai-for-science-program). Augmentation language; researcher remains the actor. Compare with Anthropic's internal W2S piece (Vector 1) for the tension.
- **Anthropic — Claude usage guides (distinct body)** — separate from the high-level Anthropic positioning above, the published Claude usage documentation is arguably the most operationally specific methodology corpus in the entire survey (see also the cross-cutting observation in §4). Five canonical pieces:
  - **"Building Effective Agents"** (Anthropic Research, December 2024). Names five composable patterns (prompt chaining, routing, parallelization, orchestrator-workers, evaluator-optimizer) and prescribes **"start simple, optimize through evaluation, and add multi-step systems only when simpler solutions fall short"** plus "human review remains crucial." An empower-not-replace-flavored stance on system design. Source: [anthropic.com/research/building-effective-agents](https://www.anthropic.com/research/building-effective-agents).
  - **Prompt engineering guides** (Anthropic docs). Structure prompting as an engineering discipline requiring empirical testing — define success criteria, build evaluations, then iterate prompt structure. Specific techniques (XML tagging, chain-of-thought, multishot, role prompting, prompt caching, extended reasoning) treated as craft skills the developer grows into. Source: [platform.claude.com/docs — prompt engineering overview](https://platform.claude.com/docs/en/docs/build-with-claude/prompt-engineering/overview).
  - **Claude Skills** (docs + October 2025 engineering blog). A `SKILL.md` format for packaging procedural knowledge for agent consumption. The framing — "analogous to putting together an onboarding guide for a new hire" — makes documentation itself a craft. Invocation control (`disable-model-invocation`, `user-invocable`) lets the human govern when the agent acts. Sources: [code.claude.com/docs — skills](https://code.claude.com/docs/en/skills); [Equipping agents for the real world with Agent Skills](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/equipping-agents-for-the-real-world-with-agent-skills).
  - **Claude Code best practices** (docs). The most explicit skill-formation engagement of any Anthropic guide. Frames the agent environment around context-window management ("the most important resource to manage"), prescribes a four-phase workflow (Explore in plan mode → Plan → Implement → Commit), names CLAUDE.md as "the agent's constitution," and closes with a "develop your intuition" section that treats expert judgment as a learnable craft. The closest any AI-lab document comes to engaging skill formation as a positive design criterion. Source: [code.claude.com/docs — best-practices](https://code.claude.com/docs/en/best-practices).
  - **Anthropic Cookbook** (GitHub). Copy-paste-ready Jupyter notebooks teaching by canonical patterns rather than prose advocacy — apprenticeship via worked examples. Source: [github.com/anthropics/anthropic-cookbook](https://github.com/anthropics/anthropic-cookbook).
- **Sébastien Bubeck** (Head of Research, OpenAI; previously Microsoft Research; co-author of "Sparks of AGI") — clearest voice for *community-standards* augmentation. Co-organized the ICML 2026 AI4Research workshop, which explicitly scopes "AI-assisted research" and excludes autonomous AI research from scope. His GPT-5 science acceleration paper (Nov 2025) flags that new mathematical results were "carefully verified by the human authors." Distinctive in shifting the question from "can AI do research?" to "what shared workflows enable researchers to use AI well?" Sources: [arxiv.org — GPT-5 science acceleration](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.16072); [ai4research-icml-workshop.github.io](https://ai4research-icml-workshop.github.io/).
- **Scite.ai** — citation intelligence platform classifying 1.6B+ citations as supporting / contrasting / mentioning. The methodological claim: citation *context* is the unit of research trust. Anti-hallucination by design (predates the ChatGPT era's concern with hallucination). Source: [scite.ai](https://scite.ai/).
- **Causaly** — biomedical research platform with *embedded* human expert review as a product feature, not a user responsibility. Manual biomedical expert review before high-stakes findings enter the knowledge graph. Targets regulatory-grade research. Source: [causaly.com](https://www.causaly.com/).
- **Connected Papers / Litmaps** — Connected Papers explicitly *not generative AI*: no LLM, no text synthesis, no summaries. Anti-hallucination by architecture. Litmaps explicitly claims "hallucination-proof" via validation of LLM outputs against literature database. Sources: [connectedpapers.com](https://www.connectedpapers.com/), [litmaps.com](https://www.litmaps.com/).

**What this vector does not address.** Skill formation as a positive design criterion. The architecture protects the human's role; it does not prescribe what the human should become better at.

### Vector 3 — Craft / skill preservation

**One-line.** AI use is a practice that can be done well or badly; the researcher is the constant; what the researcher becomes through repeated AI use is itself the methodological object.

**What separates this vector from the others.** AI use is treated as **a researcher skill, not a service**. What separates it from augmentation (Vector 2) is that the prescription lives in the *practitioner's habits*, not the tool's architecture — any tool can be used well or badly; choice of tool is one of many practice decisions. Where governance (Vector 4) addresses what AI must not do, craft addresses what the researcher must keep doing. Round 2 substantially expanded this vector with practicing scientists who reach craft conclusions independently of the AI-tools discourse. The practicing-scientist sub-camp displays a consistent frame absent from the AI-lab voices: **what the researcher must preserve** (formal verification, comprehension loops, RCT evidence, mechanistic interpretability, capacity for dissent, metacognition).

**What research looks like in practice.** AI is one of many instruments; the researcher's practice is the central object. Heavy emphasis on what the practitioner can do without AI (because that is the baseline that decays with overuse). Concrete methodological practices that recur across craft-camp members: formal verification where possible (Tao's Lean stance); short comprehension loops at every step (Howard's anti-vibe-coding); clinical-trial evidence standards before AI deployment (Topol); mechanistic interpretability as a scientific requirement (Church); preserving capacity for independent dissent (Pachter); deliberate attention to junior-researcher metacognition (Mineault); explicit calibration of how much one delegates (Mollick's centaur/cyborg + skill-atrophy warning); domain-specific pragmatism over universal claims (Wickham).

**Tools.** Tool-agnostic — the methodological move is reflective practice with whatever tool. Some craft-camp practitioners deliberately use formal verification tools (Lean, in Tao's recommendations) as the methodological answer to AI hallucination; others build the comprehension loop into their teaching (Howard's *How to Solve it With Code*; Mineault's NeuroAI course materials). The unit of methodology is the practice, not the tool.

**Players and evidence.**

- **Ethan Mollick** — *Co-Intelligence* (book, April 2024) and Substack *One Useful Thing*. The "centaur / cyborg" framing and the "always invite AI to the table" rule, paired with the skill-atrophy warning ("less accurate answers than those who were not allowed to use AI"). Source: [Centaurs and Cyborgs on the Jagged Frontier](https://www.oneusefulthing.org/p/centaurs-and-cyborgs-on-the-jagged) (September 2023).
- **Simon Willison** — "Here's how I use LLMs to help me write code" (March 2025). LLMs as "over-confident pair programming assistant"; context management is the new skill; testing "is the one thing you absolutely cannot outsource to the machine." Source: [simonwillison.net — using-llms-for-code](https://simonwillison.net/2025/Mar/11/using-llms-for-code/).
- **Terence Tao** (Fields Medalist; UCLA) — already in [reading-list.md](../reading-list.md), now also cited in step 0 for the methodology specifically. Two distinctive contributions. (a) **"AI cannot make partial progress"** — a precise structural observation that AI lacks evaluation criteria for incremental work where humans naturally operate (already foundational for cluster III in [foundation/01-ai-attributes.md](../../foundation/01-ai-attributes.md)). (b) **Formal verification (Lean) as the methodological answer to AI hallucination**, treating proof-assistant integration as a discipline that AI use should fit inside, rather than the other way around. Sources: [Dwarkesh writeup](https://www.dwarkesh.com/p/terence-tao); [Tao's WordPress AI-tagged posts](https://terrytao.wordpress.com/tag/artificial-intelligence/); [Mathematical exploration and discovery at scale (Nov 2025)](https://terrytao.wordpress.com/2025/11/05/mathematical-exploration-and-discovery-at-scale/).
- **Jeremy Howard** (co-founder fast.ai) — *Solveit* / *How to Solve it With Code* (October 2025) frames AI-assisted work in terms of Polya's 80-year-old problem-solving framework, with the explicit position that "vibe coding" creates "technical and understanding debt that will eventually become crippling." The most explicit *pedagogical* frame in this vector: AI accelerates learning only when the human maintains comprehension at every step. Source: [Solveit launch](https://www.answer.ai/posts/2025-10-01-solveit-full.html).
- **Eric Topol** (Director, Scripps Research Translational Institute; author *Deep Medicine*) — imports clinical-trial epistemology into AI methodology. Demands prospective RCTs with independent adjudication before scaling AI deployment in clinical research. Identifies "automation neglect" (doctors ignoring AI when AI is correct) and "automation deference" (doctors capitulating to AI when AI is wrong) as twin failure modes requiring strategic task separation. Distinctive in demanding randomized evidence that AI actually improves outcomes before adoption — a question the rest of the survey doesn't ask. Sources: [erictopol.substack.com](https://erictopol.substack.com/); [The paradox of medical AI implementation](https://erictopol.substack.com/p/the-paradox-of-medical-ai-implementation).
- **George Church** (Robert Winthrop Professor of Genetics, Harvard) — distinctive in (a) insisting on **mechanistic interpretability as a scientific requirement** rather than safety preference ("scientists want to know mechanisms" — black-box AI cannot ground mechanistic biology even if its predictions are accurate); (b) operationalizing AI+robot closed-loop research where AI generates hypotheses and automated wet labs test them autonomously (his role at Lila Sciences, Chief Scientist, Nov 2025). The wet-lab perspective is absent from the rest of the survey. Sources: [biohackers — AI Revolution in Science by Church](https://biohackers.media/ai-revolution-in-science-by-george-church/); [NEJM AI Grand Rounds — Machines & Genes](https://ai-podcast.nejm.org/e/machines-genes-the-future-of-ai-in-biology-with-dr-george-church).
- **Lior Pachter** (Bren Professor of Computational Biology, Caltech) — the most sociologically sophisticated craft-vector concern in the survey: AI does not just affect individual researchers but changes the **power dynamics of scientific communities** by amplifying majority approaches over heterodox dissent. "Empowerment of the majority with accelerants may serve to massively amplify flawed approaches, making it much harder for the minority opinion to prevail, even if it is correct" ("The Quickening," Feb 2026). Source: [liorpachter.wordpress.com](https://liorpachter.wordpress.com/).
- **Patrick Mineault** (NeuroAI lead, Amaranth Foundation; founding CTO of Neuromatch Academy; author of forthcoming *Good Research Code*) — the only voice in the survey explicitly grappling with the **pedagogical problem**: "using AI tools requires enough experience coding and evaluating research to have good metacognition." Junior researchers may never develop this metacognition because "AI makes the mistakes for you, invisibly." Honest about not having an answer to this mentorship challenge. Sources: [xcorr.net](https://xcorr.net/); [Claude Code for Scientists](https://www.neuroai.science/p/claude-code-for-scientists).
- **Cal Newport** (CS professor, Georgetown; author *Deep Work*, *Slow Productivity*) — articulates the **productivity-trap** position: AI accelerating administrative work crowds out deep focus rather than enabling it. Cites data showing AI tools increased administrative work substantially while reducing deep work. Source: [calnewport.com — Do We Need AI to Revolutionize Work?](https://calnewport.com/do-we-need-ai-to-revolutionize-work/).
- **Cassie Kozyrkov** (former Google Chief Decision Scientist; founder of Decision Intelligence) — frames AI as decision-support, not decision-maker; warns "the more our tools do for us, the less we seem to understand about what we're really asking them to do." Distinctive in approaching from decision science rather than engineering. Sources: [decision.substack.com](https://decision.substack.com/); [Thoughts on the AIpocalypse](https://kozyrkov.medium.com/thoughts-on-the-aipocalypse-36ed6b37e5bf).
- **Sebastian Raschka** (LLM research engineer; author of *Build a Large Language Model From Scratch*) — practitioner-educator: deep mechanistic understanding of AI is the competitive advantage; staying ahead requires reading the papers and building the models, not just using them. Source: [magazine.sebastianraschka.com](https://magazine.sebastianraschka.com/).
- **Hadley Wickham** (Chief Scientist, Posit; creator of the tidyverse) — domain-specific pragmatism. His useR! 2025 keynote (delivered *with* an LLM as a methodological demonstration) identified three concrete domains where LLMs help (code, prose, data rectangling) and flagged where domain expertise remains irreplaceable. The "I wrote this talk with an LLM" format is methodology by performance. Source: [I Wrote This Talk with an LLM — useR! 2025](https://user2025.r-project.org/program/in-person/keynotes/hadley-wickham).
- **John Carmack** (founder Keen Technologies; AGI researcher) — included as the survey's clearest outlier case: deliberately avoids AI assistance for his own AGI research because using AI to understand AI from first principles defeats the goal. Sources: Upper Bound 2025 talk; [D CEO interview (Nov 2025)](https://www.dmagazine.com/business-economy/2025/11/conversation-with-john-carmack-keen-technologies/).
- **The Royal Society** — "Science in the Age of AI" (May 2024). Warns against AI substituting for scientific judgment; calls for AI literacy investment; explicit warning about researchers being incentivized to be "good at AI" rather than "good at science." Source: [royalsociety.org — Science in the Age of AI executive summary](https://royalsociety.org/news-resources/projects/science-in-the-age-of-ai/executive-summary/).

**What this vector does not address.** A systematic methodology with foundation→evidence→guidelines structure. The position exists as essays, books, talks, podcasts, course materials, blog posts, and editorial warnings — not as a constructed methodology artifact. **The repo's own niche sits inside this vector; the construction itself appears to be open even within the vector.**

### Vector 4 — Governance / disclosure / cautionary

**One-line.** The frame is what AI must not do, what must be disclosed, and what infrastructure must precede AI integration — not how AI should be used.

**What separates this vector from the others.** The unit of methodology is **the rule, not the practice**. Where the craft camp prescribes habits to develop, governance prescribes prohibitions, disclosures, and infrastructure requirements. Round 2 added a distinct sub-position within this vector: **architectural-empirical caution** (LeCun, Apple), which produces architecture-level critique or empirical evidence about AI's limits rather than rules about AI use. The shared feature across the vector is the framing of methodology as constraint rather than positive prescription.

**What research looks like in practice.** AI use is permitted but disclosed in Methods sections or cover letters. AI cannot be an author; humans are accountable. In Science, AI output cannot be used directly as manuscript text. Funders invest in data standards and workforce training before backing methodology prescriptions. Critics flag specific tasks (hypothesis generation, peer review) as inappropriate for current AI. The architectural-empirical sub-position produces capability research showing why current AI cannot be trusted on certain classes of problems.

**Tools and infrastructure.** Editorial policies (Nature, Science, journal-specific guidelines); open-data standards (Bridge2AI); funding mechanisms (NSF AI Institutes, NAIRR); empirical capability research (Apple's "Illusion of Thinking" paper; LeCun's JEPA architecture work); critique venues (Bender's *The AI Con*, Marcus's Substack).

**Players and evidence.**

- **Nature** — AI cannot be an author; all non-trivial AI use must be disclosed in Methods. Policy established 2023, current as of 2026. Source: [Nature editorial policies on AI](https://www.nature.com/nature-portfolio/editorial-policies/ai).
- **Science** — slightly stricter: AI output cannot be used directly as manuscript text; disclosure in cover letter and methods/acknowledgments. Source: [Science journals editorial policies](https://www.science.org/content/page/science-journals-editorial-policies).
- **Emily Bender** — hypothesis generation requires human experience and bias-awareness LLMs lack; "best left to humans." *The AI Con* (Bender & Hanna, 2025) extends this to research contexts. Reference: [Stochastic Parrots paper page](https://faculty.washington.edu/ebender/stochasticparrots.html).
- **Gary Marcus** — reliability skepticism (hallucinations, no world models) applied to high-stakes research; general critique rather than research-specific methodology prescription. Source: [Marcus on AI Substack](https://garymarcus.substack.com/).
- **NIH Bridge2AI** — data infrastructure and workforce training positioned as prerequisites to trustworthy AI in biomedical research. Source: [commonfund.nih.gov/bridge2ai](https://commonfund.nih.gov/bridge2ai).
- **NSF AI Institutes** — "human-AI collaboration" as a programmatic theme; NAIRR provides compute and training access. Funding-programmatic, not methodology-prescriptive. Source: [nsf.gov/focus-areas/ai/institutes](https://www.nsf.gov/focus-areas/ai/institutes).
- **Yann LeCun / AMI Labs** — sharpest architectural critique in the survey. Autoregressive LLMs "speak without thinking," perform "System 1 reactive" processing, and cannot do multi-step planning. The intervention is upstream of the augmentation/delegation debate — current frontier AI is on the wrong architecture for science. His departure from Meta in 2026 to found AMI Labs (focused on world-model architectures via JEPA) is itself a methodological position taken in deeds. Sources: [MIT Technology Review — LeCun's new venture](https://www.technologyreview.com/2026/01/22/1131661/yann-lecuns-new-venture-ami-labs/); [amia.ai](https://amia.ai/).
- **Apple ML Research** — "The Illusion of Thinking" (June 2025) is the empirical companion to LeCun's architectural argument: frontier reasoning models "collapse" on genuinely complex problems. Apple does not state a methodology position, but the paper provides the strongest empirical basis in the survey for why human oversight cannot be optional in research-grade AI use. Source: [machinelearning.apple.com — Illusion of Thinking](https://machinelearning.apple.com/research/illusion-of-thinking).

**What this vector does not address.** A positive methodology for how AI should be used. The frame is rules and constraints, not a way of working.

### Vector 5 — Silence

**One-line.** Several institutions that would naturally opine on research methodology with AI have not staked public positions; the silence itself shapes practice by leaving it unguided.

**What separates this vector from the others.** Absence is the data point. Where the other vectors disagree about *how* research should be done with AI, this vector represents the actors that have *not engaged*. Round 2 expanded the finding substantially: well-resourced AI labs (xAI, Mistral, Cohere) and heavily-used research tools (Humata, Genei, Iris.ai) have no methodology position whatsoever, only product or company framing. The vector also includes individuals whose contribution is institutional/funding design rather than personal methodology (Collison).

**What this looks like in practice.** Researchers working in open-science / replication / preregistration cultures operate without explicit community guidance on AI tools. Existing open-science infrastructure (the Open Science Framework, TOP guidelines, preregistration templates) has not been updated to address AI-assisted analyses. Individual practice fills the gap, idiosyncratically. On the AI side, large portions of the model-providing industry route around the methodology debate entirely, building utility infrastructure for someone else to figure out how to use.

**Players and evidence.**

- **Brian Nosek / Center for Open Science** — no public position found on AI in research methodology as of May 2026. The TOP 2025 update (September 2024) addresses analytic transparency and preregistration but does not mention AI tool disclosure or AI's effect on replication. Source: [COS blog — TOP 2025](https://www.cos.io/blog/new-preprint-introduces-major-update-to-the-top-guidelines).
- **Stanford HAI** — output is descriptive measurement (the AI Index report); no found prescription on how researchers should work with AI. Source: [hai.stanford.edu/research/ai-index-report](https://hai.stanford.edu/research/ai-index-report).
- **Allen Institute for AI (Ai2)** — augmentation framing inferred from product design (OpenScholar, ScholarQA, Asta, Paper Finder); no published methodology manifesto found. Source: [allenai.org/ai-for-science](https://allenai.org/ai-for-science).
- **xAI** — explicit cosmic-mission framing ("understand the universe") with no concrete methodology beyond product capability benchmarks. The gap between the grandeur of the mission statement and the absence of any concrete methodology thinking is diagnostic. Source: [x.ai](https://x.ai/).
- **Mistral AI** — Mensch's "AI as utility" / "AI as concrete" framing — infrastructure, not methodology. Adjacent to Wissner-Gross's "Industrial Intelligence Stack" but without any explicit scientific-discovery agenda. Source: [mistral.ai](https://mistral.ai/).
- **Cohere** — enterprise workflow positioning. The most technically credentialed (Gomez co-authored "Attention Is All You Need") and the most completely silent on research methodology questions. Source: [cohere.com](https://cohere.com/).
- **Humata.ai, Genei, Iris.ai** — research tools with implicit augmentation framing but no articulated methodology beyond product features. The largest portion of the research-tool market sits here. Sources: [humata.ai](https://www.humata.ai/), [genei.io](https://www.genei.io/), [iris.ai](https://iris.ai/).
- **Patrick Collison** — co-founder of Stripe and Arc Institute, prolific writer on metascience, but no public personal methodology essay on how individual scientists should use AI. His contribution is institutional/funding design (Arc's "no predefined deliverables" model), not researcher technique. Notable as an absence at the individual level given how clearly he could opine on this. Sources: [patrickcollison.com](https://patrickcollison.com/); [arcinstitute.org](https://arcinstitute.org/).

**What absence here means.** Not necessarily that no position exists privately or is in draft; only that public canvassing did not find one. The metascience silence (COS / Nosek) remains the most consequential — the open-science community is the natural home for methodological norms on AI in research, and its absence leaves the question open. The proliferation of silent AI-side actors (xAI, Mistral, Cohere) confirms that opting out of methodology debates is a coherent corporate choice, not just oversight.

## 4. Cross-cutting observations

**Convergence.** AI is changing what's possible in research; outputs must remain attributable to humans (governance camp's bedrock); speed gains are real (delegation camp's bedrock). Augmentation is the rhetorical default — almost every player uses augmentation language even when the architecture they describe is delegation.

**Divergence.** Where execution should sit (with AI agentically — Vector 1; with the human using AI as instrument — Vectors 2 and 3). Whether the human's role is durable (frontier labs imply temporary; craft camp implies permanent; governance camp implies regulated). Whether AI hypothesis generation is methodologically respectable (DeepMind / Sakana yes; Bender / Royal Society skeptical). Round 2 added: **whether current AI is even on the right architecture for science at all** (LeCun says no, Apple's empirical work supports the skepticism, the rest of the field acts as if yes).

**What no player is claiming.** That AI use should be evaluated on whether it grows the researcher, rather than on whether it produces outputs. Round 2 makes this finding more durable: practicing scientists who *do* think about skill formation (Howard, Mineault, Pachter, Tao, Mollick, Royal Society) frame it as a **warning** or an **open problem**, never as a **positive design criterion**. The repo's "empower, not replace" stance in its explicit positive form has no direct claimant among 47 entities canvassed.

**Working scientists and AI-lab voices think about methodology differently.** This is a new round-2 finding worth tracking. Working scientists (Tao, Topol, Howard, Church, Pachter, Mineault) consistently foreground **what the researcher must preserve** — formal verification, comprehension loops, RCT evidence, mechanistic interpretability, capacity for dissent, metacognition. AI-lab voices (Karpathy, Bubeck, Willison) tend to frame methodology as **workflow design** — how to prompt, when to verify, how to iterate. The frame difference is consistent across many entities and suggests that an inquiry into AI-native research methodology has more material to draw on from working scientists than from AI labs.

**Tactical augmentation, strategic replacement.** A pattern visible in two of the most explicit entities — Wissner-Gross / PSI (tool-level "graduate student, not autopilot" + macro "Solve Everything" displacement) and Microsoft AI / Suleyman ("Humanist Superintelligence" + 12–18-month full automation prediction). The pattern is also implicit across the delegation vector more broadly: augmentation is the marketing surface; replacement is the trajectory. Naming this explicitly is useful because the contrasted position has been hard to articulate until now.

**The taxonomy is asymmetric.** Vectors 1, 3, and 4 are now well-populated; Vector 2 is mid-sized; Vector 5 is the most striking when measured against the natural authority of the silent institutions (COS / Nosek) and the size of the silent corporate players (xAI / Mistral / Cohere). Vector 3 — craft — is the largest by player count after round 2, almost entirely on the strength of practicing scientists.

**Operational methodology often comes wrapped as product documentation.** Anthropic's "Building Effective Agents" guide, Claude Code best practices, Skills documentation, prompt engineering reference, and Cookbook collectively constitute the most operationally specific methodology corpus in this survey — more concrete than the positioning of AI-lab leaders (Altman, Hassabis, Suleyman) and more specific than most academic AI-for-science position papers. The mechanism is structural: product companies have incentive to publish detailed usage guidance because it drives adoption and reduces support load, while leaders and academics have incentive to position at higher levels of abstraction. The most actionable answers to "how should a researcher actually work with AI?" are currently found in product documentation, not manifestos. **Caveat:** product-doc methodology is inherently aligned with the vendor's tool architecture — it prescribes how to work with Claude (or any specific tool) rather than how to work with AI in general. Worth tracking whether the equivalent documents from OpenAI, Google, and other labs reach similar prescriptions or diverge.

## 5. Limitations of step 0

- **47 entities is a fuller sample, not a census.** Things were still missed. The canvass should be widened in later stages, especially in domains adjacent to the repo's question (HCI augmentation lineage, cognitive psychology of skill formation, history-of-science accounts of past tool transitions, research-software-engineering literatures).
- **URLs were subagent-verified at time of fetch; nothing here is citation-grade until re-verified.** Any source that earns its way into the foundation or methodology needs to flow through `add-evidence` with its own check.
- **"No explicit position found" is a step-0 finding, not a durable absence.** Particularly for COS / Nosek and Collison, the silence should be re-checked at depth before the foundation leans on it.
- **Public positioning is what an entity *says* it does.** The gap between that and what working researchers actually do is what `evidence/interviews/`, `experiments/`, and `observations/` are for — step 0 does not substitute for them.
- **A snapshot from May 2026.** The frontier-lab positions and the heavy-AI-user voices shift on a months-scale; treating these as durable would be a mistake.
- **Working-scientist coverage is still light by field.** Round 2 added strong voices in mathematics (Tao), genomics (Church, Pachter), clinical medicine (Topol), neuroscience (Mineault), and data science (Wickham). Physics (other than Wissner-Gross), chemistry, astronomy, social sciences, and economics are still under-covered.

## 6. What this surfaces for deeper investigation

In rough priority order, for the survey's Stage 3:

1. **Elicit + Scite.ai methodology writings in depth.** Round 2 added Scite as a second tool-level cousin to "empower, not replace" alongside Elicit's "supervise the process." Both deserve a literature-note-grade read.
2. **The empirical literature on AI and skill formation.** Mineault's pedagogical problem ("metacognition cannot develop when AI hides its mistakes") is the most precise version of the question yet articulated. The Anthropic coding-skills study (already in [reading-list.md](../reading-list.md)) is one data point; the broader cognitive-psychology of skill, plus education / chess / aviation literature on tool-mediated skill formation, is almost certainly the right next territory.
3. **The centaur / cyborg vocabulary's lineage** (Kasparov → Mollick → current usage), now with **Howard's "vibe coding"** vocabulary as a second contender worth weighing. The repo currently uses neither. Stage 3 should produce a decision: adopt, refine, or contest.
4. **Whether the metascience silence is real.** Re-check whether anyone in the COS / Nosek / Ioannidis / Royal Society / preregistration world has staked a position the canvass missed. If the silence holds, decide whether the repo should address it explicitly.
5. **The "tactical augmentation, strategic replacement" pattern** (Wissner-Gross + Suleyman) — this is the closest thing the field has to a foil for the repo's empower-not-replace stance. The foundation could sharpen its argument by naming the contrasted position explicitly.
6. **Topol's clinical-RCT epistemology applied to AI methodology** — does this generalize beyond medicine? Worth investigating whether other fields could / should adopt evidence standards for AI deployment that mirror their gold standards for research.
7. **LeCun's architectural critique + Apple's empirical work** — together they constitute a substantive argument that the survey's other vectors are addressing the wrong question (how to use these tools) instead of a deeper one (whether these are the right tools). Worth a literature-note-grade read.
8. **Karpathy's agentic-engineering body of work** — Software 2.0 → Software 3.0 → agentic engineering plus Eureka Labs and the Sequoia AI Ascent 2026 piece. This is the closest cousin in the entire survey to the repo's empower-not-replace stance, framed negatively (what cannot be outsourced) where the repo frames it positively (what AI use should grow). Worth a careful read for vocabulary overlap and divergence before the foundation gains more guidelines.
9. **The Anthropic Claude usage guides as a methodology corpus** — and the parallel question of what equivalent OpenAI / Google / Mistral / xAI usage documentation looks like. If the cross-cutting observation in §4 holds (operational methodology lives in product docs), then the survey should treat these guides as a primary literature, not just promotional material. The Claude Code best practices doc in particular engages skill formation more directly than most academic sources do.
